Dates and events to plan for;

The fall refresher dates for this year are October 5th and 6th. Because of the change in CPR protocol this year an additional training day or evening is required. We need to be certified on AED this year. The additional training will be held on September 21st, 24th, 26th. The time and place has not yet been determined for the class on the 21st. Class will be held on the 24th and 26th at the Regence Blue Cross Blue Shield building, address included below along with attachment, at 6:30 p.m. This class is mandatory in order to receive your CPR for Professional Rescuer certification.

The fourth addition of the OEC manual is out this year. Every patroller is required to purchase one. You will need this manual to stay proficient regarding our protocols. This new manual will be required in order to complete this year's refresher test. The cost of the manual is $49.00. The patrol will issue a credit toward the purchase of this manual if you are a patroller in good standing. Good standing is defined as a full time patroller who has 16 patrol days, or a part time patroller who has 10 days, for the 2001-2002 season. OEC instructors fall under the same provision and are granted a slightly higher credit for their Instructor Manual set.

Every patroller is required to purchase the new CPR and AED for the

Professional Rescuer manual. The cost is $12.

All patrollers and instructors can pick up their OEC manual, OEC

Instructor manual or CPR handbook at the following;

2002 - 2003 ski season

The nights are starting to cool off and the colors are starting to change (maybe a little faster then we all would like to see). The new patrol directorship is in the midst of planning the 2002 – 2003 ski season. The OEC, IDC and CPR staff members have been working long hours to introduce the fourth edition of the “Outdoor Emergency Care” manual and the New American Red Cross CPR. The new OEC book is an incredible manual with improved pictures, supporting text and informational points of view. The new CPR manual introduces AED and new methods of providing CPR.

 

I would be amiss if we did not thank Eileen Barlage , Miles Miya , Sheila Leeds, George Felis and the instructor cadre for their dedication so early in the season to develop and implement the teaching guidelines for the various disciplines. The staff has also been busy getting the candidate OEC class underway and the Brighton ski patrol general refresher planned – the refresher will be held October 5th(Saturday) and 6th(Sunday) 2002.

 

The 2002 – 2003 ski season is approximately 8 weeks away – anticipated resort opening November 9th to the 16th. Last ski season was a unique ski year, a crazy snow fall year and an even more difficult year because of the Olympics and its demand for volunteers to staff the games. The Brighton patrol members stood out again as the patrol that in my opinion provided the largest number of volunteers to the Olympic system without the resort hosting a single Olympic event. The patrol should be proud of its continued legacy of voluntarism to the winter sports. There is another group of Brighton patrollers who are the quiet unsung heroes and heroines of the games – these are the patrollers who picked up the slack to cover the missing days and nights, so many could volunteer for the Olympics

 The patrol developed the open roster system approximately 1991 to provide a flexible method for its members to meet their skiing commitment to the resort and at the same time meet the ever demanding commitment of work and family. The open roster system required members of the patrol to maintain a minimum number of ski days during a month. The open or flexible roster system continues to work well and has provided the patrol with a unique method of covering our commitment to the resort. I would like to however, review the guidelines that provide the foundation of the open / flex roster concept. Please,

remember that each member is responsible to meet the current guidelines established in 1995.

“The Full Time Patroller”

 Each full time active member of the patrol will be required to ski 6 days per period. The member must ski on at least two different weekends each month. If a patroller does not ski the required 6 days per period, the missing ski days will be added to the next period.

Patroller did not complete of the required 6 days, would be added to the second period. This would make Joe Q. Patroller responsible for 8 days in the second period.)

If a patroller is behind in days, the patroller will be responsible to make the days up the following period. If the patroller is behind two periods consecutively the patroller will be placed on probation.

 

Probation

Probation will consist of the loss of various benefits.

  • ... family ski privileges

  • ... free ski privileges

 

When the patroller has made up the days they were behind, the probation will be lifted and the benefits reinstated.

If the patroller does not make up the days by the end of the third period, the patroller will be placed on suspension.

 

Suspension

Suspension will consist of the loss of the following

  • ... family ski privileges

  • ... free ski privileges

  • ... the receipt of vouchers for patrolling

 

When the patroller has made up the days they were behind, the suspension will be lifted and all benefits reinstated.

If the patroller does not make up the days by the end of the fourth period the patroller will be asked to leave the patrol.

 

“The part time patroller"

Revised August 2002

 The benefit package has been altered to reflect this position.

The number of part time patrollers will be limited. To be considered for this classification the patroller must be an active member in good standing and must make application to the directorship by the start of the second period of the ski year.

Term of part time classification not to exceed 9 consecutive ski periods (3 years).

The part time classification must be applied for in writing to the directorship each year.

The directorship has the option to grant or reject the application for part time classification.

The part time classification may only be applied for after every three consecutive years of full time patrolling. (3 years)

 

Required number of days:

The part time patroller will be required to ski 3 days the first and second period. 4 days within the third period (10 days total per season).

The days must be on separate weekends.

Part time patrollers will need to fulfill the same night skiing obligation.

Part time patrollers will not be able to participate in accruing patrol days during the holiday periods.

Benefits:

Ski vouchers:

The part time patroller will receive vouchers for days skied.

 Individual ski privileges:

The part time patroller will be required to pay 1/2 price for non-rooster skiing privileges.

Family ski privileges and benefits will not be extended.

If a patroller has not completed the required number of days by the end of the first period the patroller will be placed on probation.

 

Probation:

Probation will consist of the loss of the following:

1/2 price for skiing

 

When the patroller has made up the days, which they were behind, the probation will be lifted and the benefits reinstated.

 If the patroller does not make up the days by the end of the second period the patroller will be placed on suspension.

 

Suspension:

Suspension will consist of the loss of the following:

1/2 price ski privileges

The receipt of vouchers for patrolling

If the patroller does not make up the days by the end of the third period the patroller will be asked to leave the patrol.

Holidays:

2 weekdays skied during the holiday periods can count towards the required 18 days.

Night skiing changes – 2002 – 2003 ski season

 The night roster has always been an elusive, hard to nail down area of planning. It presents an area of constant concern – will all the people signed up for the night shift show? This directorship and almost every other directorship has struggled with the open night roster system, so this season we are going to try something old, but with a new twist – yup, you guessed it – night teams. Hold on a minute, don’t throw every thing in the garbage and grumble too loudly – this can be a real benefit for everyone. The night team concept is based on 15 – six member teams. Each team will be assigned various nights throughout the season when night skiing is required – yes that does mean some Fridays and Saturdays.

 

The benefit to skiing on teams is dividing the total number of nights by the team strength (6 members). The required nights breakdown as follows (not all teams will have an even disbursement of nights):

Monday night – 3 team members
Tuesday night – 3 team members
Wednesday night - 4 team members
Thursday night – 3 team members
Friday night – 4 team members
Saturday night 4 team members
 

Total nights 7 – per team – the required slots are divided among the 6-team members. Each team member will be responsible for 4 nights. Under the old method each patroller was responsible for approximately 6 nights.

Guideline for night skiing

  • Patroller must check in (present) in the Crest top patrol building by 7:00 pm on the assigned night.

  • The patroller will receive a night voucher for their duty time.

  • Special:  If the assigned patroller arrives and checks in (present) in the Crest top patrol building by 5:30 pm , the patroller will receive a Day voucher. This early check in will also be applied towards the accounting totals for senior and senior auxiliary additional vouchers.

 Note:  2 - Night vouchers can be exchanged for 1-Day voucher

 

Guideline for night roster:

 The team is responsible for providing the appropriate number of team members on the assigned nights.

Example:

3 members are required on Monday, Tuesday, and Thursday nights (2 toboggan handlers and 1 auxiliary) or (3 toboggan handlers)

4 member are required for Wednesday, Friday and Saturday nights (3 toboggan handlers and 1 auxiliary) or (4 toboggan handlers).

Shift changes between teams. If a team member is not able to cover his or her night shift from within their team, the affected team member may obtain a replacement from another team to fulfill the night ski obligation. However, it is ultimately the assigned team’s responsibility to cover their scheduled night shift.
